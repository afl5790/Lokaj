Text Analysis Project

Reading Early Internet Texts through Text Encoding and Voyant

For this project, I analyzed H.G. Wells' The Time Machine using two digital methods: XML encoding and Voyant Tools.
Encoding helped me tag important elements like characters, concepts, and repeated ideas.
Voyant Tools allowed me to see overall word patterns and trends in the novel.
Combining these two approaches helped me understand both the details and the big picture of how language and meaning are built in the story.

During the encoding process, I used tags like <name type="character"> to identify key characters.
For example, I tagged the Time Traveller as <name type="character">Time Traveller</name>.
This allowed me to track how often he appears and how the story focuses around him.
I also tagged important ideas related to time and science with <term type="concept">, such as <term type="concept">future</term> and <term type="concept">fourth dimension</term>.
Tagging these helped me see how often Wells repeats these themes and connects them to the story's structure.

After uploading my text to Voyant Tools, I started with the Cirrus word cloud.
This visualization showed that words like time, machine, little, and future appeared frequently throughout the book.
These findings supported what I had already noticed while encoding.
For example, I had tagged many descriptions of the “little people” in the future using <desc>little people</desc>.
Voyant confirmed how often the word little appeared.
This helped me understand how Wells used repetition to emphasize their fragility and contrast them with other characters.

Another Voyant tool I used was Trends.
This graph helped me see where certain words appeared more often in the story.
I noticed that the word darkness showed up much more in the second half of the book—especially when the Time Traveller encounters the Morlocks.
This matched the shift in mood and theme from light to dark, or from hope to danger.

What really stood out to me was how these two tools—encoding and Voyant—confirmed each other.
While encoding helped me focus on individual words and phrases, Voyant helped me zoom out and see larger patterns.
For example, my use of <term type="concept">future</term> early in the story matched what Voyant showed.
The word future appears more in the beginning.
These connections helped me better understand the book’s themes of time, science, and society.

Overall, this project taught me that digital tools like XML and Voyant make reading deeper.
Encoding made me slow down and pay attention to language.
Voyant showed me how word patterns work across the entire text.
Together, they gave me a richer view of how H.G. Wells used words and structure to build his message about humanity and time.

Here's an example of the XML encoding I created for The Time Machine: 


<?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?>

<chapter number="1"> 
    <title>Introduction</title> 

    <paragraph> 
        The <person name="Time Traveller" role="protagonist">Time Traveller</person> 
        (for so it will be convenient to speak of him) was expounding a recondite matter to us.  
        His pale grey eyes shone and twinkled, and his usually pale face was flushed and  
        animated with an <emotion type="excitement">eager excitement</emotion>.  
        The fire burnt brightly, and the soft radiance of the incandescent lights  
        in the <place type="structure" name="drawing room">lilies of silver</place>  
        caught the bubbles that flashed and passed in our glasses. 
    </paragraph> 

    <dialog> 
        <speaker> 
            <person name="Time Traveller" role="protagonist">
                You must follow me carefully. I shall have to controvert one or two ideas that are  
                almost universally accepted. 
            </person> 
        </speaker> 
        <speaker> 
            <person name="Filby" role="skeptic"> 
                Is not that rather a large thing to expect us to begin upon? 
            </person> 
        </speaker> 
        <speaker> 
            <person name="Time Traveller" role="protagonist"> 
                The geometry, for instance, they taught you at school is founded on a misconception. 
            </person> 
        </speaker> 
    </dialog> 

    <paragraph> 
        The <person name="Time Traveller" role="inventor">proposed</person> an  
        <invention name="Time Machine">invention</invention> that could make use of  
        this <theory>Theory of the Fourth Dimension</theory>, allowing for travel  
        through this fourth dimension—Time itself. 
    </paragraph> 

    <dialog> 
        <speaker> 
            <person name="Filby" role="questioner">
                But how can we move through Time?
            </person> 
        </speaker> 
        <speaker> 
            <person name="Time Traveller" role="visionary"> 
                Ah, that is where my machine comes in. For time, as we understand it,  
                is only a kind of Space. 
            </person> 
        </speaker> 
    </dialog> 
</chapter>
